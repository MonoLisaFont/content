Organizational friction is the drag created by incentives, structure, permissions, and decision-making. It appears when teams cannot make decisions, priorities shift without context, ownership is fragmented, or developers have responsibility without the authority to act.

This post looks at organizational friction from a developer's point of view and how broader structures affect daily engineering work.

## Sources of organizational friction

TODO:

- Decision latency.
- Misaligned incentives between teams.
- Fragmented ownership across services, products, or platforms.
- Permission systems that block normal work.
- Priority changes without explanation.
- Teams measured in ways that discourage useful collaboration.

## How to reduce organizational friction

TODO:

- Clarify decision rights.
- Align ownership with responsibility.
- Make priorities explicit and stable enough to act on.
- Give teams the permissions they need for normal work.
- Remove approval steps that do not reduce meaningful risk.
- Measure outcomes rather than only activity.

## Practical advice

TODO:

1. Identify where developers wait for decisions or permissions.
2. Clarify who owns each system, interface, or operational responsibility.
3. Ask whether approval steps reduce risk or merely move responsibility.
4. Make priority changes visible with the reason behind them.
5. Look for incentives that reward local optimization at the cost of the whole system.
